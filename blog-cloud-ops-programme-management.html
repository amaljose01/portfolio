How I run large cloud operations programmes: planning, KPIs, and keeping 10 teams aligned

Amal Jose20257 min readCloud Operations & Automation Engineer

Technical delivery at scale is as much a coordination problem as an engineering one. You can have the best automation pipelines in the world, but if ten teams across three time zones do not have a shared understanding of what is being delivered, when, and by whom — the engineering work will not land cleanly.

Over 11 years of cloud operations across large-scale SaaS platforms, I have developed a programme management approach that works for technical teams: rigorous enough to satisfy stakeholders and compliance requirements, lightweight enough that engineers do not feel buried in process. This post documents that approach.

The programme manager's job in a cloud ops context

In a traditional project management context, the PM coordinates handoffs between teams. In a cloud operations programme, the role is different. You are not managing a waterfall — you are managing a continuously operating system that also has to deliver new capabilities. The programme manager in this context owns three things:

  • Delivery visibility — at any point in time, what is the state of every workstream? What is blocked? What is at risk?
  • Dependency management — across ten teams, which team is waiting on which other team, and is that wait about to become a blocker?
  • Stakeholder communication — translating technical progress into business language for leadership, and business priorities into engineering decisions for teams.

The programme manager does not make engineering decisions. They make sure the right people are making the right decisions at the right time, with the right information.

Programme structure: the workstream model

For large cloud operations programmes, I organise work into workstreams rather than a single flat backlog. Each workstream has:

  • A single owning team and a named technical lead
  • A clearly scoped objective with a measurable outcome
  • A set of defined dependencies on other workstreams
  • Weekly status: Green / Amber / Red with a one-line rationale
WorkstreamOwnerObjectiveStatus
Tenant MigrationPlatform OpsMigrate 200 tenants to new runtime by Q3Green
ObservabilitySREReduce MTTR from 45min to 15min via new alerting stackAmber
Security HardeningSecOpsZero critical findings on Q3 pen testGreen
Cost OptimisationFinOps + InfraReduce cloud spend 20% without SLA impactRed

The RAG (Red/Amber/Green) status is not bureaucratic theatre. It is a communication tool. Red means: this needs a decision or resource that I as a programme manager cannot resolve — it needs leadership attention. Amber means: this is at risk and I am managing it, but you should know. Green means: no action needed from stakeholders.

The weekly rhythm

Large programmes fail when communication happens in bursts — a big kickoff, then silence, then a panic update when a deadline is missed. The cadence I maintain:

Daily: async status update

Each workstream lead posts a two-sentence Slack update to the programme channel: what moved forward yesterday, what is blocked today. Takes 2 minutes to write, takes 3 minutes for the programme manager to read across all workstreams. Blocking issues surface within 24 hours, not at the weekly meeting.

Weekly: cross-team sync (45 minutes, structured)

Not a status readout — the async updates handle that. The weekly sync is for three things only:

  1. Dependency review — which teams need something from which other team in the next two weeks?
  2. Blocker resolution — items that are Amber or Red and need cross-team decision
  3. Upcoming milestones — what is going live in the next two weeks and what is the go-live checklist?

Everything else stays in the workstream-level standups. The cross-team sync ends early if there is nothing to resolve. Engineers notice when you respect their time.

Bi-weekly: executive update (one page)

Leadership does not need technical details. They need to know: are we on track? What are the risks? What decisions do we need from them? The bi-weekly executive update is a single page with five sections: programme health (RAG), milestone summary, risks and mitigations, decisions needed, and a one-line outlook for the next two weeks.

The rule of executive communication: If a leader has to ask a follow-up question to understand the status, the update failed. Write for the reader, not for yourself.

KPIs that actually drive behaviour

Most cloud operations programmes track output metrics — tickets closed, deployments completed, incidents resolved. These are trailing indicators. By the time they look bad, the programme is already off track. I track a mix of leading and lagging indicators:

Leading indicators (predict future health)

  • Dependency resolution rate — what percentage of cross-team dependencies are resolved before they become blockers? Below 80% is a warning sign.
  • Estimation accuracy — how close are workstream estimates to actuals, week over week? Consistent underestimation means the team is under pressure and not surfacing risks early.
  • Blocker age — how long does a Red item stay Red before it is resolved? Blockers older than 72 hours without a resolution path need escalation.

Lagging indicators (measure outcomes)

  • Milestone delivery rate — percentage of milestones delivered on the committed date. Target is above 85%. Below 70% means planning is broken.
  • Scope change rate — how often is scope added to in-flight workstreams? Frequent scope additions indicate requirements were not clear at kickoff.
  • MTTR trend — for operational programmes, is mean time to resolve incidents trending down? This is the ultimate measure of whether the programme is delivering operational improvement.

Dependency management in practice

Dependencies are where large programmes die. Team A is waiting for a config from Team B. Team B did not know Team A needed it until Team A mentioned it in the weekly sync — three weeks after Team B finished the relevant work and has since moved on. The knowledge is now buried in someone's head.

The dependency tracking model I use is simple: a shared table with four columns.

This table is reviewed in every weekly sync. Any dependency with a "Required by" date less than two weeks out that is not Done gets a named owner and a daily check-in until it is resolved.

Go-live readiness: the checklist that travels

Every major delivery milestone — a migration wave, a new service launch, a platform upgrade — goes through a go-live readiness checklist. Not as a bureaucratic gate, but as a structured way to surface the questions nobody thought to ask until 11pm the night before go-live.

The checklist covers:

  • Technical readiness — smoke tests passing, rollback procedure tested and documented, monitoring and alerting configured for the new surface
  • Operational readiness — on-call engineer briefed, runbooks updated, support team notified of changes that affect their workflows
  • Communication readiness — affected stakeholders notified, customer communication drafted (if applicable), change request approved
  • Rollback criteria — explicit decision: under what conditions do we roll back, who makes that call, and how?

The checklist is completed by the workstream lead, reviewed by the programme manager, and signed off by the engineering lead 48 hours before go-live. Any open items at 48 hours either get resolved or become explicit risks accepted by leadership.

The thing they do not teach you

Programme management literature is full of frameworks — PRINCE2, SAFe, OKRs. What it does not teach is the most important skill: knowing when to escalate and when to absorb.

Not every Amber item should go to leadership. Not every team delay is a programme risk. The programme manager's judgement — developed from understanding the technical context, the team dynamics, and the business stakes — is what determines which problems need senior attention and which ones need to be quietly resolved at the working level before they become noise.

That judgement is what separates a programme manager who adds value from one who adds meeting overhead. It is also what takes years to build. The frameworks are just scaffolding.
